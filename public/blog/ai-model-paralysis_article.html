There Are 47 AI Models. I Have Credits for 12 of Them. I Still Don't Know Which One to Use.

When your org gives you access to Claude, Gemini, ChatGPT, Kimi, and a dozen others - each with different models, effort levels, and credit caps - how do you actually decide? A framework for choosing AI models that maximises accuracy without burning through your budget in three days.

By Divyansh Singh July 2026 ~12 min read
01 · The Problem

The Paralysis Is Real

Last Tuesday I spent 20 minutes staring at my terminal trying to decide which AI model to use for a code refactoring task. Not because I didn't know how - because I had too many options.

Three tiers of Claude models. Multiple Gemini variants. Several ChatGPT options. Kimi for long context. DeepSeek and Llama for self-hosting. And that's before you factor in the effort levels - low, medium, high, xhigh, max - each consuming different amounts of tokens and producing different quality outputs.

My org had given me access to all of them, with monthly credit caps on each provider. The freedom was paralysing. Use the wrong model and I'd burn through credits on a task that didn't need it. Use too cheap a model and I'd waste time fixing hallucinated code. Twenty minutes of decision paralysis is twenty minutes not shipping.

"The paradox of choice isn't theoretical when you're burning $0.50 per API call and your monthly budget resets in six days."

This isn't a complaint about having too many tools. It's an observation that the AI landscape has fragmented faster than the mental models developers use to navigate it. We went from "use GPT-4 for hard stuff, GPT-3.5 for cheap stuff" to a 12-dimensional decision matrix in 18 months.

02 · The Real Problem

Why Model Selection Actually Matters

The stakes are higher than they look. Pick wrong and one of three things happens:

  • You overspend. Running Opus 4.8 at max effort on a task that Flash 2.0 could handle burns through your monthly budget in a week. Then you're stuck with the free tier, rate-limited to hell, for the rest of the month.
  • You underspend and pay with time. A cheaper model gives you plausible-looking code that breaks in production. You spend two hours debugging what a better model would've caught. The API cost was lower; the engineer cost was higher.
  • You spend decision energy instead of coding. Every task becomes a meta-task: "Which model should I use for this?" That cognitive overhead compounds. By afternoon you've spent more time choosing models than using them.

The root issue is that most developers don't have a consistent decision framework. We're guessing, or copying what worked last time, or defaulting to whichever model we saw mentioned on Twitter recently. That works until your budget runs dry or you ship a bug that a $0.30 API call would've prevented.

Real Example I used Claude Opus 4.7 at high effort to generate a regex pattern for URL validation. Took 8 seconds, cost $0.40, worked perfectly. A week later I caught myself doing the same thing - and realised Gemini Flash could've done it in 2 seconds for $0.02. The output would've been identical. I'd wasted $0.38 because I didn't stop to think.
03 · The Landscape

What You're Actually Choosing Between

Let's map the territory. These are the models most orgs are giving devs access to as of mid-2026:

Most orgs provide access to models across several tiers:

Tier 1: Fast & Economical

Models optimised for speed and cost. Good for straightforward tasks with clear right answers - code generation, simple refactors, test case creation. Examples include Claude Haiku, Gemini Flash, and similar fast-tier options from other providers.

Tier 2: Balanced

The middle ground - capable enough for most coding tasks, priced reasonably. This is where you'll spend most of your budget. Examples include Claude Sonnet, GPT-4o, and Gemini Pro variants.

Tier 3: Maximum Capability

The expensive, slow, powerful models reserved for complex reasoning. Claude Opus, GPT o1, and similar top-tier models live here. Use sparingly - these justify their cost on hard problems but waste budget on simple tasks.

Specialised Models

Some models have specific strengths: long context windows, multimodal capabilities, or particular reasoning styles. Kimi handles massive documents well. Open-source options like DeepSeek and Llama offer self-hosted alternatives. Match these to specific needs rather than using them as general-purpose tools.

04 · The Framework

A Decision Framework That Actually Works

Here's the system I've used for the past three months. It's not perfect, but it's reduced my decision time from minutes to seconds and cut my monthly AI spend by roughly 40% without sacrificing output quality.

The core insight: task classification matters more than model specs. Instead of memorising which model is best at what, classify your task into one of four buckets, then use a preset model + effort combo for that bucket.

Bucket 1: Mechanical Tasks

Examples: writing boilerplate, generating test cases, formatting JSON, regex patterns, simple refactors, docstring generation.

Model tier: Fast/economical tier (the cheapest option from your available providers).

Effort: low or default.

Why: These tasks have clear right answers and no ambiguity. Speed and cost matter more than deep reasoning. Fast-tier models excel at structured, deterministic tasks - generating hundreds of test stubs or boilerplate in seconds for pennies. Save your expensive model credits for problems that actually need them.

Example Prompt · Mechanical Task
# Use: Gemini Flash 2.0 (low effort)

Generate TypeScript test stubs for all exported functions in 'utils.ts'.
Use Vitest syntax. Include describe blocks grouped by function.

Bucket 2: Ambiguous or Creative Tasks

Examples: API design decisions, naming things, architectural tradeoff discussions, explaining unfamiliar codebases, writing human-facing documentation.

Model: Balanced-tier models (mid-range options from your providers).

Effort: medium.

Why: These tasks need judgment, not just correctness. Sonnet is excellent at understanding context and making reasonable recommendations. Pro is slightly better at creative naming but weaker on code-heavy analysis. For pure coding tasks, Sonnet wins. For docs or explanations, Pro is competitive and cheaper.

Bucket 3: Complex Reasoning or Debugging

Examples: tracing a subtle bug through multiple files, understanding why a complex algorithm fails, reviewing architecture for hidden issues, refactoring tangled legacy code.

Model: Top-tier models (the most capable, expensive options).

Effort: high to max.

Why: These are the tasks where a cheap model will confidently give you wrong answers. Opus is the most reliable here - it's slower and costs more, but it catches edge cases the others miss. o1 is great for algorithmic problems but occasionally overthinks simple bugs. Gemini Thinking is underrated for deep debugging but has a smaller context window.

When to Use Max Effort

Reserve max effort for tasks where a mistake costs more than the API call. Debugging a production incident? Max. Refactoring payment logic? Max. Writing a regex for a contact form? Absolutely not.

As a rule: if the code touches money, auth, or user data, or if you're about to commit something you won't review again for weeks, use max. Everything else can live at medium or lower.

Bucket 4: Long Context or Document Analysis

Examples: summarising long transcripts, finding patterns across 10+ files, analysing full git diffs, processing documentation sites.

Model: Models with large context windows or strong document analysis capabilities.

Effort: medium.

Why: Kimi has a 200k+ token context window and handles massive inputs without choking. Opus is better at synthesis but more expensive. Pro sits between them - decent context, good summarisation, cheaper than Opus. Pick based on input size: under 50k tokens, use Pro. Over 100k, use Kimi.

05 · Budget Management

Optimising for Credit Caps

The framework above assumes unlimited budget. Real orgs give you caps. Here's how to adapt:

Track Spend by Task Type, Not by Day

Most devs track AI spend by checking their dashboard once a week and panicking when it's at 80%. Better approach: log every API call by task bucket. At the end of the week, look at the distribution.

If 60% of your spend is on Bucket 1 (mechanical tasks), you're overspending. Those should be 10-15% of budget, max. Shift those tasks to Flash or Fable and your budget stretches.

Front-Load Expensive Models Early in the Month

Don't ration Opus evenly across 30 days. Use it liberally in week one when your budget is full. By week three, you'll have fewer complex tasks (because you've already debugged the hard stuff) and can coast on cheaper models.

Set Hard Caps Per Task Type

I allocate my monthly AI budget (let's say it's $100 as an example) roughly like this:

  • 15% on mechanical tasks (Fable, low effort)
  • 50% on ambiguous/creative tasks (Sonnet, medium effort)
  • 30% on complex reasoning (Opus, high effort)
  • 5% buffer for emergencies

When a bucket hits its cap, I either switch to a cheaper provider (Gemini Flash for mechanical, GPT-4o for reasoning if I've burned through Opus) or defer non-urgent tasks to next month. Sounds rigid, but it prevents the "burn 80% of your budget in week one on regex generation" disaster.

Budget Alert System I wrote a 30-line bash script that wraps my AI CLI calls and logs cost to a local JSON file. Every morning it shows me: budget used, budget remaining, projected end-of-month spend at current rate. When projected spend hits 90%, I get a desktop notification. Took 20 minutes to build, saves me from budget surprises every month.
06 · Testing Your Models

Building Your Own Model Profile

The tier system gives you a starting point, but every model has quirks. Some are conservative and won't suggest risky refactors. Others are aggressive and will confidently propose solutions that don't compile. Some handle ambiguity well. Others need crystal-clear prompts.

You need to profile your available models yourself. Here's the fast way:

Week One: Baseline Testing

Pick five representative tasks from your actual work. One mechanical, two balanced, two complex. Run each through every model in your toolkit at medium effort. Log the results: which succeeded, which failed, which were close but needed tweaking.

This gives you a rough map. You'll spot the models that consistently nail certain task types and the ones that consistently miss.

Week Two: Effort Calibration

Take the models that did well on complex tasks. Run the same tasks again at low, medium, high, and max effort. Log cost and quality. Find the inflection point - where does more effort stop improving output?

For most models, there's a sweet spot. Below it, quality drops fast. Above it, you're burning credits for marginal gains.

Month One: Pattern Recognition

By the end of the first month, you'll have a personal dataset. Some fast-tier models will surprise you - handling tasks you assumed needed balanced-tier. Some expensive models will disappoint - costing 5x more for identical output.

This data is worth more than any article or benchmark. It's based on your actual workload, your prompting style, your code patterns. Trust it over marketing claims.

07 · Common Mistakes

The Mistakes Everyone Makes

I've made every one of these. You probably will too. Here's how to avoid them:

Mistake 1: Using One Model for Everything

Defaulting to your "favourite" model for every task is like using a sledgehammer for every nail. You'll burn budget on tasks that didn't need it. Diversify.

Mistake 2: Penny-Pinching on Critical Tasks

The opposite mistake: using a fast-tier model to debug a race condition because "it's just a quick question". Cheap models give you plausible-sounding nonsense on hard problems. I've wasted hours following confident-but-wrong debugging advice from the wrong tier. The API call saved 20 cents; the wasted time cost $50 in opportunity cost.

Mistake 3: Ignoring Effort Levels

Most devs don't even know effort levels exist. They use default for everything. But a balanced-tier call at high effort is often better than a top-tier call at low - and cheaper. Experiment with effort before jumping to a bigger model.

Mistake 4: Not Logging What Works

You'll forget which model you used for that task that worked perfectly three weeks ago. I keep a plaintext log: date, task, model, effort, outcome. Takes 10 seconds per call. After a month you'll have a personal dataset of what works for you.

The Log That Changed My Workflow

My log has 200+ entries now. The patterns were surprising: fast-tier models handled 40% of tasks I'd been using balanced-tier for, with identical results. Top-tier was worth it for debugging but overkill for architecture discussion. Effort level often mattered more than model choice.

The log takes 5 minutes a week to maintain. It's saved me probably $400 in wasted API calls.

08 · Quick Reference

The 30-Second Decision Tree

When you're about to make an AI call and don't want to think about it:

  • Is there a clear right answer? (Boilerplate, tests, formatting) → Fast tier, low effort.
  • Is it ambiguous or creative? (API design, naming, docs) → Balanced tier, medium effort.
  • Are you stuck on a hard problem? (Deep bug, complex refactor) → Top tier, high effort.
  • Is the input huge? (Long diffs, documentation) → Long-context model, medium effort.
  • Does it touch money, auth, or user data? → Top tier, max effort, no exceptions.

That covers 90% of tasks. For the other 10%, use your log.

09 · The Meta-Problem

The Real Problem: This Will Get Worse

The model landscape is accelerating. In six months there will be 20 major models, each with new capabilities we'll have to evaluate. The framework above won't scale forever.

What will scale: task-based classification. New models will come and go, but tasks stay the same. Mechanical tasks will still be mechanical. Ambiguous tasks will still be ambiguous. When a new model drops, you don't need to re-learn your entire workflow - you just test it on your four buckets and see where it fits.

The companies that figure out model routing for developers will win. Imagine a tool that auto-selects the right model based on your prompt and your budget cap. You write "debug this race condition" and it routes to top-tier at high effort. You write "generate test stubs" and it routes to fast-tier at low. Zero decision overhead.

That tool doesn't exist yet. Until it does, frameworks like this are the best we have.

"The endgame isn't developers memorising model capabilities. It's developers never having to think about models at all."

10 · What to Do Monday

What to Actually Do With This

If you take one thing from this article, make it this: classify your tasks, then preset your models.

Spend 20 minutes this week defining your four buckets and picking a default model + effort combo for each. Write it down. Stick it on your wall. For the next month, use those defaults religiously and log the outcome of every call.

At the end of the month, look at your log. You'll see patterns - tasks where the cheap model was fine, tasks where you should've used the expensive one, tasks where effort level mattered more than model choice. Adjust your defaults based on that. Repeat monthly.

The goal isn't to find the perfect model for every task. The goal is to remove the decision paralysis so you can spend your time coding instead of comparison-shopping AI models at 2am.

And if your org only gives you access to one model? You're reading the wrong article, but also: you're lucky. Constraints are clarifying.
